16. Nonlinear Models & GAMs

16.1 Generalized Additive Models (GAMs)

GAMs assume that features have smooth, additive non-linear relationships with the target: $y = \beta_0 + f_1(x_1) + f_2(x_2) + \dots$. They use splines to learn local curves without sacrificing global model interpretability.

17. Tree-Based Models

17.1 The Extrapolation Limit

Tree-based regressors (Decision Trees, Random Forests, Gradient Boosting) predict targets by averaging leaf samples. Consequently, they **cannot extrapolate** beyond the range of targets seen in the training data.

⚠️ Math Fact: If maximum crop yield in training is 30 tons/acre, the forest will predict 30 even if you give it optimal temperature and 100 bags of fertilizer. Linear models (like OLS) do not suffer this limit.

18. Gradient Boosting for Tabular Regression

Gradient Boosting constructs trees sequentially, where each new tree is fit to predict the negative gradients (residuals) of the loss function calculated in the preceding step. Boosted trees represent the state-of-the-art for structured tabular data.

20. Target Transformations & Skewed Targets

For targets with long right tails (like infrastructure costs), OLS is dominated by extreme points. Applying log1p ($y_{\text{new}} = \log(1+y)$) stabilizes the variance. Revert predictions using expm1 ($y = \exp(\hat{y}_{\text{new}}) - 1$) to prevent exponential bias.

21 & 22. Multi-Output & Hyperparameter Tuning

Optimize parameters with Bayesian Optimization (e.g. Optuna). For multiple related targets (like infrastructure cost and duration), use RegressorChain to predict duration first, then use predicted duration to help forecast cost. This avoids leakage during inference.

24. Model Interpretation

24.1 SHAP (Shapley Additive exPlanations)

Based on cooperative game theory. SHAP measures how much each feature pushes a prediction away from the global base value. It satisfies properties like local accuracy and consistency, providing reliable local explanations.
